Alvys Wosinton

"I hear that one does ablutions with her ankles somewhere in the vicinity of her ears."

The cart, filled to toppling with hay and pulled by a single mule, moved along the forest track at a walking speed. Ralph Miles drove, nearly enveloped by the hay piled behind him, chaff in his beard and furring the top of his hat. At the moment he was asleep, hunched into himself and snoring, the reins held loosely in his hand, but the mule knew the way along the double track without his help.

John le Lung, fifteen and a nephew of Ralph, walked along the embankment beside the cart, swatting at daisy heads with a switch. Every so often the mule would stop—she was old and spavined to the point of deformity—and when she did, John would smack her on the rump with his switch and she would glare at him briefly before lurching back into motion. John would make a handsome man once his features all grew into themselves, but for now his Adam’s apple nearly led his nose and he walked like he was falling down.

The forest around them was young, but dense, alive with songbirds of all sorts. Every so often, a stone chimney grew up between the trees, evidence that this place had not always been wooded; there had been a village here once, before the great Pest. How quickly the wild reasserted itself. We’re all renters, that’s all, Ralph’s mother used to say. His Lordship in his castle, the Bishop and his miter, and the peasant most of all. God have mercy on all us renters.

As the wagon rolled down the gentle slope towards the ford in the creek, it came around a bend and there, sitting spry as a goat atop a boulder, was Brother Gilbert le Armurer. He was thin, with sleepy-lidded eyes and the sort of narrow, pointed beard a grass snake might grow, if it were able.

“Ho there!” Brother Gilbert hollered.

Ralph awoke with a start, yanked on the mule’s reins, and the entire endeavor came to a creaking, swaying halt. The friar stood, hiked his filthy robes up to his shanks and hopped off the boulder and onto the path.

“Is that you, Ralph Miles?” Friar Gilbert said.

“Well, it were this morning, when I saw myself in the pisspot,” Ralph said, then yawned into his hand.

“And hello to you as well, John,” Friar Gilbert said. The boy waved shyly from behind the wagon.

“Give you a ride, brother? We’re to the market in Schrosberie,” Ralph said.

“Good luck, it is. There I sat, asking if it were wiser to wear through the thin soles of my sandals, or wait here for the Lord to provide. And here you are. I’m to Schrosberie myself. I’ve been invited to sermonize the guilds,” Friar Gilbert said, accepting a hand up from Ralph into the cart.

“I saw the rest of you blackfriars this morning, mounted up on their asses with dust in their wake. They passed me and the boy two days ago. Said they wanted to be in Schrosberie before the market opened,” Ralph said.

“Yes, well I was detained,” Brother Gilbert said, pushing the hay back enough to settle in against it.

“Detained at the inn?” Ralph said, clucking the mule into motion again. The wagon heaved up out of a rut with a clatter.

“As it happens,” Brother Gilbert said, drawing his black cloak snug over his white robes.

“Ministering to the innkeep’s eldest, I suppose? I hear that one does ablutions with her ankles somewhere in the vicinity of her ears.”

“I’m sure I don’t know what you’re talking about,” Brother Gilbert said, then, changing the subject, “Do you know what I found just recently?”

“That the innkeep’s daughter had a new child sired sometime around last year’s market? One born perhaps clasping the rosary?” Ralph said.

“Not at all. I found this. It was with a pile of scrolls the prior wanted me to sort.”

Brother Gilbert reached into his cloak and produced a small book. It was black and unadorned, hardly larger than the span of his palm.

“I do recognize that,” Ralph said, a smile cracking his beard, “It has been a long time. What? Twenty years? More?”

“What is it?” John said, chinning up over the lip of the wagon to peer at the book.

“This is the diary of a man your uncle and I used to know. A curious man,” Brother Gilbert said.

“Curious is too dainty a word. Full-moon mad’s more to the tune,” Ralph added.

"Alvys Wosinton, the Mad Sorcerer," Brother Gilbert said.
